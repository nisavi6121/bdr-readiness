Methodology

How the readiness score works — every decision explained

Model Overview

The BDR Readiness Score replaces a binary MQL threshold with a transparent, decomposed 0–100 readiness signal. It deliberately does not use is_mql, mkto_lead_score, mql_date, lead_status, or created_date as scoring inputs — those fields reflect legacy process state, not genuine buying readiness.

Weights are differentiated by entity type because contacts carry richer account context than leads, making account fit a stronger signal for contacts.

Leads: Final Score = 0.75 × Engagement + 0.15 × Account Fit + 0.10 × Profile Fit
Contacts: Final Score = 0.60 × Engagement + 0.25 × Account Fit + 0.15 × Profile Fit

Component 1 — Engagement (leads 75%, contacts 60%)

Engagement is the dominant signal because it measures real behavioural intent. It is computed per campaign type so that a single attended event is not diluted by a hundred automated sends.

Per-type weights

  • Event: 30% — highest-intent, human attendance signal
  • Webinar: 25% — strong interest, lower commitment than in-person
  • Content Syndication: 20% — demonstrated research intent
  • Telemarketing: 10% — direct conversation, moderate intent
  • Email: 10% — broad and often automated; discounted accordingly
  • Advertisement: 5% — weakest signal; passive impression

Recency decay

Each event is weighted by exp(−k × age_days) with a 30-day half-life (k = ln(2) / 30). An event 30 days ago counts at 50%, 60 days ago at 25%.

Volume cap

Volume is scored as count / type_cap, clipped to [0, 1]. Type caps (Event=3, Webinar=5, Email=10, etc.) prevent spam campaigns from dominating. The recency and volume scores are multiplicative — both must be non-zero for a type to contribute.

Automation filter

Events with member_status = Sent are excluded before computing the per-type signal. This prevents automation-inflated histories (DQ-8) from inflating the score.

Raw signal to normalised score

The per-type contributions are summed into a single raw_engagement_signal value. Because recency and volume are multiplicative, a record that maxes out both dimensions across multiple high-weight campaign types can accumulate a raw signal several times larger than the next-best record — Robert Sanchez's raw signal of 4 034 is 2.76× the Lead ceiling of 1 461.

The raw signal distribution is heavily right-skewed and bimodal:

  • ~52% of leads / ~45% of contacts have a raw signal ≤ 1 — essentially no genuine engagement
  • A dead zone from roughly 10–200: very few records sit here because the multiplicative formula means weak recency or weak volume wipes out a campaign type's contribution entirely
  • The engaged cluster (200–1 000+) represents records that hit both dimensions across at least one high-weight type
  • ~5% exceed the ceiling and are clipped to 100

This shape is why the normalisation method matters. A pure percentile rank would assign the median record a score of 50 — implying average engagement — when its actual raw signal is near zero. The 95th-percentile ceiling correctly gives that record ~0, preserving the true meaning of the signal.

Lead vs Contact fairness

Normalisation is done within entity type separately (Lead ceiling ≈ 1 461, Contact ceiling ≈ 1 825 in the current dataset). This means a lead is only compared against other leads, and a contact against other contacts — the two populations are not competing against each other for the top scores.

Component 2 — Account Fit (leads 15%, contacts 25%)

Account fit measures how well the record's company matches the ICP, weighted by a segment multiplier encoding data confidence.

Sub-signals

  • ICP Industry: 50 pts — Software, Financial Services, Healthcare, Telecom, Energy
  • Named Account: 25 pts — pre-identified strategic target
  • Employee Count: 15 pts — 500–10 000 = full; 100–50 000 = 0.65×; else 0.25×
  • Intent Score: 10 pts — third-party account-level intent (0–100 normalised)

Segment multipliers — account data source quality

Segment reflects how reliable the account fit signal is, not the intrinsic worth of the record. Lead segments are based on ZoomInfo enrichment availability; contact segments on lead origin linkage.

  • L1 (lead matched to a CRM account): 0.90
  • L2 (no CRM match; enrichment available): 0.55
  • L3 (no CRM match; no enrichment): 0.45
  • C1 (has lead origin — native CRM record, full account linkage): 1.00
  • C2 (no lead origin; enrichment available): 0.55
  • C3 (no lead origin; no enrichment): 0.45

Named account flag is already a sub-signal in the raw account fit score (25 pts) and is excluded from segment assignment to avoid double-counting.

Component 3 — Profile Fit (leads 10%, contacts 15%)

Profile Fit = (job_level_score + job_persona_score) / 2

Job level scores

  • C-Level: 1.00 · VP: 0.85 · Director: 0.70 · Manager: 0.50 · Individual Contributor: 0.30

Job persona scores

  • CISO: 1.00 · Technical Buyer: 0.85 · Financial Buyer: 0.70 · Influencer: 0.50 · Non-Prospect: 0.00

Imputation

Missing values are imputed with the within-entity-type median derived score — never the population mean, never mixing leads and contacts.

Hard Blockers (Flagged tier)

Hard blockers are orthogonal to the score. Any record with one or more of these receives the Flagged tier regardless of score:

  • Non-Prospect persona (Competitor, Employee, Vendor)
  • Account-level do-not-contact
  • No longer with company

Soft Flags (do not affect tier)

These are surfaced in the BDR action note but do not change the tier assignment:

  • Email opted-out: no email channel — call or engage at event only
  • Bounced email: use phone or LinkedIn; email undeliverable
  • Broken conversion link: engagement history may be incomplete (DQ-1)
  • Automation-inflated engagement: capped at Follow Up; signal reliability noted

Tiers

Thresholds are differentiated by entity type — leads score structurally lower because engagement weight is higher and leads tend to have shorter histories.

  • Call Now (Lead ≥ 65 / Contact ≥ 70): priority outreach within 24h
  • Follow Up (Lead ≥ 35 / Contact ≥ 40): schedule within 5 business days
  • Nurture: long-term sequence
  • Flagged: review blocker before any outreach

Why Rules-Based?

No labeled outcomes (meetings booked, opportunities, revenue) exist in this dataset. A supervised ML model would fit noise without ground truth. A transparent rules-based model is more honest, auditable, and can be calibrated against BDR team intuition directly.
